## SPECIFIC AIMS

Transthoracic echocardiography (TTE) is the frontline bedside cardiac modality, yet its diagnostic value depends on continuous operator regulation of probe pose, contact force, and acoustic coupling on a moving, compliant chest wall [1]. Demand keeps rising while the sonography workforce faces elevated vacancies and work-related musculoskeletal discomfort, leaving bedside acquisition the access bottleneck [2, 3]. Robotic ultrasound, image-quality assessment, and cardiac acquisition prototypes exist individually, not as one runtime system under realistic disturbances [4, 5]. The barrier is runtime integration, not component invention: image-derived feedback has not been qualified as a control-ready signal coupled with adaptive force-pose control in a low-latency, contact-aware loop that attains and maintains TTE views [5].

The long-term goal is supervised shared-autonomy robotic assistance that expands access to high-quality bedside cardiac imaging. The **objective** is to test whether closed-loop coupling of runtime image-derived feedback with adaptive force-pose control can attain and maintain an initial three-view TTE subset under increasingly realistic conditions [6]: parasternal long-axis (PLAX), mid-papillary parasternal short-axis (PSAX), and apical four-chamber (A4C). The **central hypothesis** is twofold. First, a characterized runtime signal can guide bounded local pose refinement to attain the target views from controlled near-view starts while preserving independently judged diagnostic quality. Second, coupling that refinement to adaptive contact-force regulation will improve maintenance duration, recovery time, and degradation under disturbance relative to pose-only image feedback. The **rationale**: diagnostic-quality acquisition requires co-regulating image quality, pose, and contact force, shown individually, not coupled at runtime [4, 5].

**Aim 1. Establish a control-relevant image-quality feedback signal and pose-response envelope for runtime TTE acquisition.** In offline and benchtop characterization, this aim tests whether a deep-learning view-adequacy model yields a low-latency, stable, pose-sensitive signal fit for closed-loop control rather than offline classification [4]. Primary endpoints are sensor-to-formed-signal latency, stability, graded decline under coupling loss, and local pose-response in a bounded envelope; agreement with expert-rated adequacy is secondary. This qualifies the signal as the control input for Aims 2 and 3.

**Aim 2. Test whether bounded image-guided force-pose control can reliably and efficiently attain diagnostic-quality selected TTE views.** This aim couples the Aim 1 signal to bounded derivative-free pose refinement and adaptive force-pose control, evaluating attainment in a static TTE-relevant phantom. The primary endpoint is attainment success rate, with time-to-view a supporting efficiency contrast; blinded diagnostic-quality rate at attainment guards against trading quality for speed [6]. The key contrast, integrated control versus pose-only image feedback, isolates whether adaptive force coupling adds a static-attainment benefit.

**Aim 3. Evaluate whether integrated closed-loop control improves view maintenance, perturbation recovery, and robustness across staged validation.** To test this integration claim under realistic conditions, a programmable TTE-relevant motion phantom imposes respiratory, rib-interference, acoustic-window, and coupling-loss disturbances; exploratory healthy-volunteer feasibility under operator and safety oversight follows, with claims restricted to tested conditions [5]. The primary endpoint is maintenance duration above the runtime signal threshold with blinded diagnostic-quality confirmation, compared against pose-only image feedback; recovery time is supporting, and force-safety bounds constrain any claimed gain.

The central contribution is runtime perception-control integration: image-derived feedback and adaptive force-pose control as one acquisition capability. The controller's signal stays separate from the blinded diagnostic-quality standard, so scalar-feedback failures are measured, not hidden. The work claims no sonographer replacement, autonomous diagnosis, clinical deployment, or patient-outcome improvement; it establishes the acquisition-side foundation for broader views, disturbances, and clinical populations.

## BACKGROUND & SIGNIFICANCE

Echocardiography is the frontline cardiac diagnostic modality, informing triage and management across emergency, intensive care, and operative settings [1]. Its reach is limited less by interpretation than by acquisition. Diagnostic-quality TTE acquisition is structurally operator-dependent: the probe must be threaded between ribs while contact force and acoustic coupling are continuously adapted to a moving, compliant chest wall under cardiac and respiratory motion [1]. The task is iterative and physical, the operator first locating a usable acoustic window, then sustaining it by adjusting probe pose and contact pressure in response to real-time image-quality feedback. This skill is also scarce. Sonographer demand has outpaced the training pipeline: from 2011 to 2021, open positions rose 36.3% while graduates increased 23.5% [7], and the vacancy rate reached 12.4% in 2025 [2]. The constraint is structural rather than temporary, and it reaches the patient, because access to a timely, diagnostic-quality cardiac study can hinge on whether a skilled operator is available to acquire it [2]. Better interpretation tools, scheduling, or distance-bridging cannot remove the bedside acquisition step. Acquisition-side robotic assistance is therefore positioned to extend diagnostic access and reduce operator dependence under workforce constraints, in support of trained sonographers rather than as a replacement [1].

The robotic ultrasound field has responded along three fronts, each demonstrating one ingredient a closed-loop TTE system would need. Telerobotic and robot-assisted echocardiography shows that robot-mediated probe manipulation under continuous human control is feasible for remote acquisition, solving a workflow and access problem rather than runtime perception-control integration [8]. Runtime AI guidance to a human scanner improves novice performance on standard echocardiographic views, showing that real-time acquisition-side feedback materially aids scanning even when a person still executes the motion [9]. Non-cardiac autonomous robotic ultrasound shows that anatomy-specific autonomy with image-driven, force-aware adaptation can reach high performance in human subjects, though in anatomies with broader acoustic windows, less respiratory and cardiac motion, and less rib occlusion than TTE [10]. Embodied execution, runtime image-quality feedback, and integrated force-aware adaptation thus all exist, but each matures in a research class adjacent to transthoracic cardiac acquisition, and the cardiac-targeted work that does exist remains fragmented.

Cardiac-targeted prototypes are themselves heterogeneous: simulation-stage standard-view navigation frameworks, single-view robotic acquisition pipelines [11], and force-pose-image scanning robots whose force loop is admittance-compensated rather than driven from image quality. Runtime image-quality feedback, contact-force regulation, pose adjustment, and disturbance handling each appear in some cardiac system, but in different ones and under different evaluation regimes. Integration demands more than that these capabilities exist; it demands that they co-function on the same patient under one disturbance budget, which component demonstrations spread across separate systems cannot establish. The field is therefore non-empty but unconsolidated, populated by multiple prototypes that are each rigorous within scope, yet no single cardiac system has consolidated and characterized this integration for diagnostic-quality TTE acquisition under realistic transthoracic disturbances.

The barrier, then, is not that perception and control have never been coupled to reach cardiac views. The most tightly integrated cardiac prior art, Zakeri's deep-reinforcement-learning visual-servoing system, already closes a learned image signal onto a hybrid force/position controller and autonomously reaches its target cardiac views [12]. The barrier is what that integration leaves unestablished. The image-derived feedback driving the loop has not been qualified as a control-ready signal, the loop is judged against its own internal target rather than an independently rated diagnostic-quality endpoint, and view maintenance and recovery under realistic transthoracic disturbance remain untested. No system yet couples a characterized image-quality signal to adaptive force-pose control as a low-latency, contact-aware runtime loop that attains and sustains diagnostic-quality TTE views under those disturbances.

Three structural reasons explain why closure has not been shown, and each maps to one face of that gap. First, perception latency, update rate, and uncertainty are rarely treated as explicit control-design variables in robotic TTE acquisition. Deep-learning view scoring can produce a continuous quality measure [13], but few studies establish that the signal is timely and stable enough to drive safe contact-aware adaptation across the bandwidth of cardiac and respiratory motion. Second, force, image search, and pose optimization are decomposed across separate subsystems rather than jointly adapted from one image-quality signal, and where force is image-informed it tracks a feature-keyed force setpoint rather than adapting to graded image quality [12]. Tang's cardiac scanning robot, for instance, integrates image-servo orientation with axial admittance compensation but keeps its force loop admittance-driven rather than image-driven, and targets ROI localization and window optimization rather than diagnostic-view endpoints [14]. Third, prior cardiac work concentrates on attaining a view rather than sustaining it under perturbation. Echo-Robot regulates constant-force compliance only along the probe z-axis, decoupled from its image-quality classifier, and terminates upon view acquisition with patient motion left as future work [15]; Zakeri's system is validated only in simulation and on a static phantom, again deferring patient motion [12]. Compounding all three, the realistic disturbance stack of rib and lung interference, acoustic-coupling drift, and respiratory motion has been handled piecewise rather than as a coordinated bundle under transthoracic conditions, a pattern visible in respiratory compensation demonstrated in isolation [16].

Resolving this barrier would establish the acquisition-side foundation for the next class of robotic-acquisition studies. The non-cardiac autonomous robotic-ultrasound literature already shows that anatomy-specific autonomy with image-driven, force-aware adaptation is tractable in easier-anatomy classes [10]. Those integration concepts remain usable analogues for cardiac work even where anatomical asymmetry prevents direct transfer: intercostal access, rib shadowing, narrow acoustic windows, and combined cardiac and respiratory motion. This grounds the step as continuous with an active research arc rather than a discontinuous leap, narrowing the open question to whether the runtime coupling closes under transthoracic conditions rather than whether closed-loop acquisition is plausible in the abstract. Closure would replace an assumed controller with an acquisition-side controller class whose behavior under realistic transthoracic disturbances is characterized rather than presumed. That turns subsequent staged evaluation into a well-posed engineering question, and reduces how much a timely, diagnostic-quality cardiac study depends on the availability of a skilled operator. That integration is the precondition for broader view subsets, more demanding disturbances, and clinical populations, framed as enabling future supervised shared-autonomy bedside acquisition assistance, not as a substitute for that broader translational program.

## INNOVATION

Closing a perception-control loop to reach cardiac views has been demonstrated [5, 12]. What remains open is not integration itself, but three questions: whether the feedback signal is control-ready, whether the controller is held to an external diagnostic standard, and whether the loop survives realistic transthoracic disturbance. Prior systems treat image quality as a measure read after the fact, as guidance displayed to a human operator [4], or as a learned feature target a controller servos toward. In each case the signal is consumed as a black box rather than characterized as a runtime control input, and the loop satisfies its own internal target rather than independently judged diagnostic quality. Integration is the vehicle. The distinctive contribution is the qualified signal, the external diagnostic standard, and the disturbance-tested maintenance claim.

First, the project establishes image quality as a control-relevant signal rather than a simple scalar label. Deep-learning view-adequacy models already run at low latency to guide operators in real time [4], but their output is used as a label or a display, not characterized as something a controller can safely consume. The project converts that output into a runtime signal with a defined latency-stability-pose-response operating envelope and a graded response to loss of acoustic coupling. This tests directly whether the signal has usable local structure over probe pose, rather than treating a black-box score as if it supplied an analytic gradient. The signal then becomes a quantity the field can reason about and reuse, with its control-readiness established directly. An end-to-end policy's task-level convergence never shows this: reaching a target reveals nothing about whether the underlying signal is timely, stable, locally informative, and graded enough to drive safe adaptation [12].

Second, the project drives force and pose from that characterized signal as one adaptive loop, then judges that loop by an external standard. Realized cardiac systems either keep force and image quality on separate loops, or close the loop by servoing toward an internally defined target [12]. This work instead asks whether the views the coupled loop attains pass independent, blinded diagnostic-quality criteria, a harder and more meaningful test than whether a controller reaches its own target. Because the loop is compared against less-integrated alternatives that remove force regulation, image feedback, or both, the design isolates whether image-guided pose refinement and image-quality-modulated force regulation each contribute to independently judged attainment. The separation is deliberate: the runtime signal drives the controller and the stopping rule, but scalar-high, diagnostic-quality-low cases are scored as control-proxy failures, not successes.

Third, the project asks what prior cardiac acquisition has largely left open: not whether a view can be reached, but whether it can be held and recovered. The closest systems report how efficiently a view is found, then stop once it is attained. Sustained maintenance and recovery under realistic transthoracic disturbance are rarely the measured outcome. The project makes maintenance duration the primary measured endpoint under programmable disturbance and across stages of increasing realism, with recovery after an imposed loss as a supporting endpoint and force safety as a within-bounds constraint. This turns robustness into a measured quantity comparable on matched terms, and isolates the behavior most likely to require true force-pose integration rather than pose refinement alone. If these predictions hold, the field gains a characterized, independently judged, disturbance-tested foundation for closed-loop cardiac acquisition: the acquisition-side basis on which broader view subsets, more demanding disturbances, and eventually clinical populations can be studied. The contribution is that foundation, not a deployable system, an autonomous diagnosis, or a substitute for the sonographer.

## APPROACH

### Aim 1. Establish a control-relevant image-quality feedback signal and pose-response envelope for runtime TTE acquisition.

Aim 1 qualifies the image-quality signal before any downstream controller consumes it. The signal under test is a scalar runtime score combining target-view identity with view adequacy over the selected three-view subset, reported frame by frame or after temporal aggregation. The aim asks whether that score is timely, stable, graded under coupling loss, and locally pose-informative enough to serve as a control input. It is not asked to adjudicate diagnostic quality, nor to provide a global map for whole-chest search. That separation holds throughout the proposal: in Aims 2 and 3 the signal drives bounded controller actions and stopping logic, while blinded expert review, not the signal, judges whether an acquired view is diagnostically adequate.

**Rationale.** Deep-learning view-adequacy scoring is mature offline, with continuous quality scores and real-time operator guidance [4, 13], yet its runtime control properties remain uncharacterized. A controller needs more than a correct label: it needs bounded latency, stable behavior under held conditions, a monotone response when coupling degrades, and usable local structure when probe pose changes. Recent deep-reinforcement-learning robotic echocardiography steers a robot toward cardiac views on a phantom [12], yet reaching a view does not establish them. The key hidden risk is a flat-then-cliff score field: if the signal is near-binary across pose, a controller described as image-guided refinement would silently degrade into bounded search until it enters the slope. Aim 1 makes that assumption testable before the signal closes any loop.

**Design and methods.** One shared estimator serves all three target views, so the same signal is characterized at the benchtop and evaluated offline against archival clips. It takes B-mode frames and emits a view-identity confidence and a target-conditioned adequacy score. The runtime control scalar is their product, s = P(target view in frame) x target-view adequacy, high only when a frame is both the intended view and an adequate instance of it; a confident wrong view or a blurry right view drives s low. The estimator is fixed by this input-output behavior, not by a network: a reference instantiation pairs a pretrained backbone with identity and adequacy heads, with the architecture left as a reference choice. Aims 2 and 3 consume s as the attainment threshold s*, so attainment means right-view-and-adequate, and the central failure mode is s exceeding s* while the blinded readers still judge the view inadequate. Training and validation use public, expert-annotated datasets, each under its published labeling protocol: CAMUS as the primary image-quality-grade anchor, TMED-2 for view-type labels and a large unlabeled pool, EchoNet-Dynamic for large-scale A4C representation, and Unity Imaging for PLAX and quality coverage. Data are split at the patient or recording level, view-balanced where feasible, with no train-evaluation leakage and a held-out test set reserved for final signal characterization.

The score is characterized as a sensor, not a label, through four behaviors of the same scalar s, each against a control-eligible boundary: sensor-to-formed-signal latency, signal stability, graded coupling response, and local pose-response structure. The two substrates carry complementary properties. Latency is benchtop-only: only the live acquisition pipeline, which is timestamped end to end on a dual-host bench [16], yields the full sensor-to-formed-signal chain; offline replay times only the inference-and-aggregation leg. Stability draws on both. The static phantom gives the clean noise floor, the median absolute deviation of the 0-to-1 scalar s over held-constant windows across aggregation settings; offline real-anatomy replay supplies physiological variation, reported separately. Graded coupling response and local pose-response are benchtop-only: both need controlled, repeatable manipulations archival clips cannot provide. Coupling response uses an ordered sweep of five degradation levels from full gel contact to full decoupling; pose-response uses arm-applied probe-frame offsets from a known-good window out to a committed search bound. Offline replay carries the model-facing checks: secondary agreement with expert-rated adequacy, and off-target view rejection using TMED-2 view labels.

| Property | Control-eligible boundary |
|---|---|
| Latency | Median at or below 100 ms, 95th percentile at or below 150 ms, update rate at or above 10 Hz. The 30 to 50 ms range is a future within-cardiac-cycle target, not committed. |
| Stability | Median absolute deviation of s at or below 0.05 within held-constant windows. This deviation is the stability noise floor referenced by the pose-response criterion. |
| Graded coupling response | A significant monotone decline in s: negative rank correlation of magnitude at or above 0.7 (p < 0.05), or a significantly negative ordinal mixed-effects slope. |
| Local pose-response | A significant ordered decline in s as offset grows to the search bound, plus a capture basin where improving and worsening moves are distinguishable above the stability noise floor. If only coarse directional structure is present, Aim 2 uses bounded local sweep rather than continuous-ascent language. |

Contact force is logged in the benchtop studies as a validity condition, not an Aim 1 endpoint, confirming that perturbations occur in a representative contact regime within a conservative force and pressure bound. That bound is derived from obstetric probe-force literature [17], so it serves only as a conservative reference, not a claimed TTE force range. The actuator and control-execution leg is measured downstream in Aim 2; Aim 1 stops at the controller-ready signal and its operating envelope.

**Expected outcomes.** Aim 1 yields a latency-stability operating curve, a graded coupling-response curve, and a local pose-response map. These curves also select the temporal-aggregation operating point for downstream control, rather than assuming the fastest frame-level output is best. The deliverable is a control-eligible operating envelope: inside it, s serves as the bounded image-quality input for Aims 2 and 3; outside it, the signal is not used for closed-loop control. The primary prediction is that the estimator meets all four control-eligible criteria while retaining secondary agreement with expert-rated view adequacy and correct off-target rejection. Meeting it qualifies the signal for downstream control.

**Potential problems and alternatives.** Aim 1 has explicit failure modes: the signal could prove too slow, too unstable, binary rather than graded under coupling loss, or flat across pose. The subtler failure is that it proves no more control-relevant than a simpler, non-integrated signal. Aim 1 therefore tests it against three baselines: a binary contact gate [18], an offline-classifier-only output (the same estimator on archived clips), and a feature-engineered image-entropy scalar [10]. Each is compared on every property it can support; latency is excluded for the offline output by construction. The runtime-control justification fails only if a baseline matches the signal on every property relevant to downstream control; a single-property match narrows the claim rather than defeating it. A second set of rules narrows scope without changing the endpoints. If agreement with expert-rated view adequacy falls below an analogous reference band (inter-rater Cohen's kappa of roughly 0.62 to 0.64 [19]), the view subset narrows to the PLAX and A4C pair, preserving the parasternal and apical acquisition logic while avoiding an unsupported three-view claim. If local pose-response structure is weak, the downstream control claim narrows from derivative-free image-guided refinement to a bounded scripted local sweep with image-quality stopping. If no usable pose-response range exists, the runtime-control claim is withdrawn and Aim 1 stands as a defensible benchtop and offline negative characterization. These rules prevent Aim 2 from inheriting an untested pose-gradient assumption.

**Rigor.** The analysis plan is pre-specified. The primary contrast is the runtime signal against the strongest non-integrated baseline. Effect sizes and confidence intervals are reported alongside p-values, with the trend estimator (Spearman, ordinal, or mixed-effects slope) fixed in advance by decision rule. The independent unit is the session for latency, the windowed segment for stability, the coupling-level-by-traversal cell for graded response, and the pose-offset-by-session cell for pose response, with within-session repeats modeled as nested. The secondary expert-agreement check is reported as Cohen's kappa under a pre-specified, blinded, two-reader rubric, and off-target rejection as target-versus-off-target discrimination on view-labeled clips. The runtime signal carries unchanged into Aim 2 only if it satisfies these operating-envelope criteria and is re-confirmed at the Aim 1 to Aim 2 handoff. There, a single operating point on the latency-stability-pose-response curve is selected to drive the closed-loop controller within the characterized capture basin.

### Aim 2. Test whether bounded image-guided force-pose control can reliably and efficiently attain diagnostic-quality selected TTE views.

Aim 2 tests the first closed-loop consequence of Aim 1: whether the runtime signal improves view attainment when it drives bounded derivative-free pose refinement and, in the integrated arm, contact-force adaptation. A static TTE-relevant phantom isolates attainment reliability and efficiency before Aim 3 adds dynamic disturbance. Four arms run from matched starts, and the primary contrast is integrated closed-loop control versus pose-only image feedback: both share the same image-driven pose refinement and differ only in whether contact force also adapts to image quality. Open-loop scripted scanning and force-only stabilization are the no-image comparators.

**Rationale.** Because diagnostic-quality acquisition requires co-regulation of probe pose and contact force, the prediction is that the integrated loop, not image feedback or force control alone, drives attainment [5]. A static cardiac phantom gives the repeatable trial structure attribution needs. Echo-Robot's roughly 78% A4C attainment from a 6 cm by 6 cm randomized-start zone sets the scale: the expected level for pose-only image feedback, which the integrated arm must exceed by the pre-specified minimum meaningful difference [15].

**Design and methods.** Trials are organized into matched start-pose blocks. Each block draws one randomized bounded start pose inside the Aim 1 capture basin, the local neighborhood where the runtime signal reliably improves toward the target view, and all four arms run from that same start so arm differences are not confounded by start-pose difficulty. The phantom is a commercial adult TTE training phantom whose chest-wall and rib geometry partially constrains the acoustic window and yields standard views gradable by expert review [15]. The approach into the basin is a scripted geometric move onto a known surface, so the signal never drives global search; generalizing this to an unknown patient surface, as shown for vascular access by AURA-CVC [20], is future work, not built here.

A collaborative manipulator holds the probe and senses the contact wrench through a wrist force/torque sensor or joint-torque estimation, running all four arms under software-configurable impedance or admittance control [16]. The controller consumes the temporally aggregated Aim 1 signal s and that wrench. Because s is a scalar with magnitude but no direction, the controller cannot follow a pose gradient; it samples s at small bounded probe-frame moves and keeps those that raise it, a derivative-free local search confined to the capture basin and the force-safety envelope. If Aim 1 resolves only coarse directional response, a bounded scripted sweep with image-quality stopping meets the same behavior; controller form follows the Aim 1 result. Accepted end-effector increments are converted through inverse kinematics into joint-space commands. Compliance control regulates contact force on a separate channel. The integration variable is whether s also modulates that force: in the integrated arm a low signal can adjust commanded force within the envelope to recover coupling; in pose-only image feedback the identical pose search runs with contact force held fixed and decoupled from the image. On a static phantom the two no-image comparators cannot steer toward a view they did not start on. The signal s is logged for all four arms as the common yardstick but drives control and the s* stopping rule only in the two perception arms. Two criteria govern each trial, kept separate. Controller attainment is defined by the runtime signal: a single threshold s* is the stopping criterion, and because s is the product of view identity and adequacy, the near-0.7 placeholder is a joint bar on both factors, not a single-adequacy 0.7, pending the operating point selected from Aim 1's curve. Attainment requires s to cross and hold at or above s* for a 2 s interval rather than at a single best frame, following temporal-aggregation precedent in cardiac robotic acquisition [11]. Diagnostic quality is adjudicated separately. Two independent blinded readers score each acquired view as adequate or inadequate against view-specific BSE [21] and ASE [6] criteria: correct plane and orientation, complete chamber anatomy, no foreshortening, and correct mid-papillary PSAX level. Reader agreement is reported as Cohen's kappa [22]. Controller attainment and diagnostic-quality rate at attainment therefore stay separate endpoints.

Aim 2 inherits the operating point selected from Aim 1's latency-stability-pose-response curve only after re-confirmation on the phantom. The actuator and control-execution latency leg, from controller-ready signal to commanded actuation, is measured here to close the loop budget that Aim 1's sensor-to-signal half left open [16]. Safety is matched across arms: an identical hardware-bounded safety floor, force cap, and abort rule apply to all conditions, with active force regulation only inside that envelope [16, 22]. Trials end after sustained attainment, the 120 s search-window cap, or a force-safety abort. Aim 2 runs roughly 50 matched start-pose blocks balanced across the selected views, all four arms per block, for about 200 arm-trials.

**Expected outcomes.** The primary endpoint is attainment success rate: the fraction of trials reaching sustained attainment within the 120 s window. The integration benefit is declared only if the lower bound of the 95% confidence interval for the integrated-minus-pose-only difference exceeds a pre-specified minimum meaningful difference of 10 percentage points, set as the smallest gain that would justify the added force-coupling complexity. Time-to-view is the supporting efficiency contrast against the same comparator [15]. Diagnostic-quality rate at attainment guards against trading quality for speed and counts as preserved only if the lower bound of its 95% confidence interval stays above -15 percentage points, read from the interval rather than a powered equivalence test [21]. Secondary endpoints are trial-to-trial repeatability [23], contact-force variance, and contact-force peak [16]. The expected outcome is phantom-stage evidence that image-guided pose refinement supports bounded attainment, with the integrated contrast deciding the force-coupling benefit.

**Potential problems and alternatives.** No phantom reproduces the full TTE acoustic-window stack [24], so Aim 2 treats the static partial-rib phantom as an attainment substrate, not a substitute for the dynamic and volunteer stages. Elevated inter-trial variance is handled by modeling trial heterogeneity rather than collapsing the primary contrast to a point estimate [23]. Mid-papillary PSAX may add within-window rotation and level-selection demands beyond PLAX and A4C; if it proves unsupported and Aim 1's two-view fallback has not already fired, the subset narrows to PLAX and A4C rather than forcing an unsupported three-view claim [21].

The main falsifying result is pose-only image feedback matching the integrated loop on attainment, which is plausible because Echo-Robot reaches the field-baseline anchor using image-driven pose and z-axis-only force compliance decoupled from its image-quality CNN [15]. If the lower confidence bound does not clear the minimum meaningful difference, Aim 2 shows no phantom-stage force-coupling benefit, narrows the attainment claim to pose refinement, and passes force coupling to Aim 3. A second pattern is scalar-quality divergence: s reaches s* and holds, but blinded review fails the view on the same criteria. That case scores as controller attainment with diagnostic-quality failure, and if frequent it narrows the interpretation to a failure of the control proxy. Two inherited contingencies remain: the multi-axis platform is a build-time cross-aim lock [15], and failed signal re-confirmation on the phantom routes all perception arms to the Aim 1 fallback.

**Rigor.** The primary contrast, success threshold, quality guardrail, and failed-trial accounting are fixed before data collection. Attainment success is reported as an effect size with a 95% confidence interval against the pre-specified minimum meaningful difference, and the diagnostic-quality margin as a guardrail [23]. The independent unit is the matched start-pose block, with within-block repeats nested; run order is randomized and block sequence counterbalanced across arms [15]. The primary contrast is powered through matched-block pairing, not an unpaired per-arm guarantee. The two blinded readers are the same across all four conditions and blinded to arm assignment [22]. Matched hardware and identical safety limits attribute attainment differences to the control law, not to the platform, safety architecture, or unequal search opportunity. Failed trials stay in the analysis: a search-window timeout and a force-safety abort are attainment failures kept in the denominator and tabulated per arm, so no arm wins by excluding timeouts, low-quality attainments, or aborts. The latency leg, comparators, and force-control behavior carry into Aim 3's maintenance and recovery tests.

### Aim 3. Evaluate whether integrated closed-loop control improves view maintenance, perturbation recovery, and robustness across staged validation.

Aim 3 carries the proposal's main integration claim: whether the bounded image-guided pose refinement that attains views, once coupled to adaptive contact-force regulation, also maintains and recovers them under disturbance while staying inside the force-safety envelope.

**Rationale.** Prior cardiac robotic acquisition has focused on reaching a target view, not on how long it stays usable, how fast it recovers after loss, or whether adaptive force regulation helps beyond pose correction [15, 25]. Aim 3 tests a bounded prediction: compared with pose-only image feedback, the integrated loop maintains diagnostic-quality views longer, recovers them faster after imposed loss, and, while comparable at low respiratory severity, degrades less as severity rises. This is the more demanding force-coupling test: respiration moves the acoustic window itself, so maintenance is not holding a fixed pose but tracking a moving target on a compliant chest wall, which adaptive force regulation can do and pose correction alone cannot. Because no single substrate reproduces every acquisition constraint, the claim is staged: phantom experiments test the primary maintenance and recovery hypothesis, and healthy-volunteer experiments test feasibility and supervised operation on real anatomy.

**Design and methods.** The phantom stage is the primary experiment. Once a target view is attained and held at the in-basin pose, controlled disturbance is applied while maintenance and recovery are logged. The committed core disturbance is respiratory motion. The Aim 2 adult TTE training phantom is mounted on a programmable single-axis stage, with severity set by commanded displacement that is fixed independently of the runtime signal and of the arm under test [16, 24]. Severity is swept at roughly 5, 10, and 15 mm peak-to-peak near 0.25 Hz, plus an irregular-breathing condition. Rib-like shadowing, limited window geometry, and coupling loss are modular additions, added only if reproducible and otherwise reported as not built rather than used to support a partial claim. Recovery uses a defined imposed-loss event: a discrete, supra-respiratory excursion of the same stage, calibrated to carry the target out of the acoustic window and drive the signal below s*. Recovery is clocked from the s* down-crossing, and the known loss time aligns recovery timing across arms and separates the acute event from the continuous respiratory motion used for maintenance.

All four Aim 2 comparator arms run on the phantom under disturbance. Because only the two image-feedback arms can steer the probe back toward the target, the maintenance and recovery contrast is integrated closed-loop versus pose-only image feedback, with open-loop and force-only as floor references. Pose-only is the strongest comparator: it preserves image-guided pose refinement but removes adaptive force coupling. The same safety floor, force cap, and abort criteria from Aim 2 apply across arms, so differences trace to the control law, not the safety architecture. The runtime signal controls the robot only within its characterized latency-stability-pose-response envelope. Its operating point and capture basin are re-validated under the committed respiratory motion before Aim 3 and before volunteer use, since the static Aim 1 characterization can shift once the window moves; actuator latency is inherited from the Aim 2 dual-host platform [16]. The runtime signal does not grade diagnostic quality: two independent blinded readers apply the same society-anchored view-adequacy criteria used in Aim 2, so the control proxy never adjudicates its own target. Scalar-high, quality-low segments are scored as diagnostic-quality failures, not successful maintenance [21].

The volunteer stage is exploratory feasibility, not clinical validation [22]: roughly 8 to 12 healthy adults recruited across sex and body habitus. The view subset follows Aim 1, narrowing the full three-view subset to PLAX and A4C (PSAX opportunistic) if the expert-agreement fallback applied. Because the phantom's scripted geometric move cannot reach a volunteer window, a supervising operator places the probe near the window and hands off to the robot for fine refinement and maintenance. This bounds the feasibility claim to supervised near-window placement, the shared-autonomy division this work targets. Only the integrated and pose-only arms run in volunteers, under natural breathing, with no robot-commanded imposed-loss applied. Scans are supervised under the inherited safety stops (force cap, hard abort, comfort, operator), with a force-safety envelope derived from phantom behavior and conservative literature anchoring rather than a validated transthoracic limit, since none exists [17].

**Expected outcomes.** As predicted, the integrated arm should hold views above s*, with blinded diagnostic-quality confirmation, longer than pose-only and recover lost views faster, inside the force-safety envelope. The two arms should match at low severity and diverge as severity rises, so the benefit widens with disturbance. Volunteer results should agree in direction if the phantom behavior translates, but remain descriptive and unpowered.

**Potential problems and alternatives.** The main falsifying result is that pose-only image feedback matches the integrated closed-loop on maintenance duration, with a matching recovery contrast. That would show runtime force coupling adds no demonstrable maintenance-side benefit in the tested regime, a bounded negative finding independent of Aim 2, which tests attainment rather than maintenance. The result cannot be manufactured by self-grading or selective accounting, because blinded review confirms diagnostic quality and every endpoint keeps its denominator; a match reads as lack of demonstrated advantage, not equivalence. A signal that stays high while blinded quality fails is scored as a failure, not a win on the controller's own scalar. The largest feasibility risk is the disturbance rig, since no phantom reproduces the full TTE disturbance taxonomy [16]. If the optional disturbance modules cannot be built repeatably, the aim narrows to the respiratory-motion core and reports them as unbuilt, leaving the maintenance, recovery, force-safety, and severity-degradation claims intact. If the signal fails the per-stage re-confirmation gate, Aim 3 follows the inherited Aim 1 fallback.

**Rigor.** Phantom trials use randomized starts, matched arm exposure, randomized run order, and time-aligned logs, on the order of two dozen or more trials per arm within each disturbance condition. Maintenance duration is timed from attainment onset as the interval the signal stays at or above s* under continuous respiratory motion. Sub-threshold dips under about 0.5 s are tolerated; a longer drop ends the maintained state, and intact holds at the 60 s observation window are right-censored. Recovery time runs from imposed-loss onset to re-attainment, the signal re-crossing s* and holding the same 2 s sustained-attainment interval used in Aim 2 [11] within a 30 s budget. Trials that do not recover are retained as non-recoveries, not assigned a maximum.

Both are right-censored time-to-event outcomes, summarized per arm by Kaplan-Meier and compared with a shared-frailty mixed-effects survival model that absorbs the nested within-condition repeats. Maintenance is the lead primary endpoint; its model effect is the hazard ratio for loss of maintenance, integrated versus pose-only. The restricted mean survival time difference to 60 s is the interpretable companion and carries the pre-specified minimum meaningful difference, since it stays defined under the heavy 60 s censoring. Following the Aim 2 confidence-interval device, the benefit is declared only if the lower bound of the 95% confidence interval on that difference clears the minimum meaningful difference. Recovery is supporting and does not co-gate: recovery time among recovered trials and recovery success across all loss-subjected trials each carry their own minimum meaningful difference under the same rule, and severity degradation is the change in the maintenance effect across the sweep. Each endpoint preserves its denominator, so no arm can appear better by attaining, maintaining, or recovering rarely, and force-safety descriptors (peak force, contact-force variance, dwell within the envelope) remain a within-bounds constraint rather than a non-inferiority test. Volunteer analysis is descriptive and treats participants as the independent unit; the diversity goal is a commitment, not a result, and a force-safety abort during a hold right-censors maintenance. Target-view status that blinded readers cannot resolve is scored conservatively as a quality failure. Aim 3 completes the experimental arc, testing whether the integration characterized in Aim 1 and used for attainment in Aim 2 also sustains diagnostic-quality acquisition under realistic disturbance, without claiming clinical deployment or validation.
